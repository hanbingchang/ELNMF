Overview of ELNMF

The ELNMF model constructs a graph-regularized non-negative matrix factorization framework by integrating a hybrid graph that simultaneously incorporates spatial proximity and gene expression similarity, thereby effectively capturing both spatial structural information and transcriptional patterns in spatial transcriptomics data. Building upon this framework, an ensemble learning-driven consensus clustering strategy is further introduced to integrate clustering information derived from multiple latent representations, improving the stability and robustness of spatial domain identification. The overall workflow of ELNMF is illustrated in Fig. 1 and consists of four main stages.First, the raw spatial transcriptomics data are preprocessed by removing low-expression genes, performing normalization and logarithmic transformation on the gene expression matrix, and selecting highly variable genes based on expression variance. These procedures reduce technical noise while preserving biologically informative features.Second, a graph-regularized non-negative matrix factorization model is constructed based on the hybrid graph that integrates spatial and expression information. By employing different latent representation dimensions, the gene expression matrix is factorized multiple times to generate a collection of low-dimensional latent representations, enabling the characterization of intrinsic tissue structures at multiple scales.Third, under the ensemble learning framework, clustering is performed on each latent representation to generate a set of base clustering results. An unsupervised evaluation function combining the Silhouette Score and CHAOS metric is then employed to identify high-quality clustering solutions. The selected clustering results are subsequently integrated through a consensus clustering strategy to construct a consensus similarity matrix, which is further processed using hierarchical clustering to identify spatial domains. By aggregating clustering information from multiple latent representations, this procedure effectively reduces the sensitivity of the model to parameter selection and random variations, thereby enhancing the robustness and reliability of spatial domain identification.
Finally, the learned cell embeddings and gene embeddings obtained from matrix factorization are utilized for downstream biological analyses, including spatial visualization based on clustering results, cell–cell communication inference using cell embeddings, and Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis based on gene embeddings. These analyses further validate the biological relevance and interpretability of the representations learned by ELNMF.
